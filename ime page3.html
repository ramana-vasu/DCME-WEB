INTRODUCTION TO IME:


Management plays an important role in every organization. Management is a universal and dynamic process of all economic and non-economic activities in an organization. It is not merely restricted to a factory (or) office. It is necessary for business firms, government organisations, education, health services and many other organisations. 'Management' is the process of planning, organizing, staffing, leading, directing, and controlling the activities of an organization. To achieve the goals of an organization, it is necessary to guide, direct and co-ordinate the efforts of others. Thus, management is the art of getting things done through people to accomplish desired goals and objectives using available resources efficiently and effectively. Therefore, it can be said that no organisation can enjoy a successful existence and survival without competent management. Whereas, Industrial Management' is concerned with the organizational process, which includes policies and procedures, strategic planning, setting objectives, managing the resources such as human and financial resources needed to achieve the objectives, and finally measuring the results. It integrates manpower, materials, and other factors in productive ways and utilizes all the available resources to the maximum extent.

Business:


Business: Business literally means 'state of being busy" in economic activities. Business is an occupation, profession, (or) trade, (or) is a commercial activity which involves providing goods (or) services in exchange for profits. Moreover, business is related to continuous and regular production and distribution of goods and services for satisfying human wants. Thux, business is an economic activity, involving the regular production lor) purchase of goods and services for sale, transfer and exchange with the aim of eaming profit
DEFINITION OF BUSINESS:
1. Lewis Henry defines business as, "human activity directed towards producing for) acquiring wealth through buying and selling of goods".
2. Stephenson defines business as, "The regular production (or) purchase and sale of goods undertaken with the objective of earning profit and acquiring wealth through the satisfaction of human wants".
Business activities may be broadly classified into two categories. The activities of the business may be broadly classified into two categories, namely:
1. Industry
2. Commence
1. Industry: The production side of business activity is referred to as 'industry'. An industry is a group of companies that are related based on their primary business activities. Industry is termed as an economic activity concerned with the processing of raw materials and the manufacture of goods in factories.
I DEFINITION OF INDUSTRY:
• An industry is "a group of competitors producing substitutes that are close enough that the behaviour of any firm affects each of the others either directly (or) indirectly". -Michael Porter
Classification of Industries: Industry may be classified into three categories, such as:
(i) Primary Industries:
(a) Extractive industries such as the mining industry, coal mines, the oil industry etc., and
(ii) Genetic industries like poultry, plant nurseries etc.,
(ii) Secondary Industries:
(a) Manufacturing industries, namely textile, sugar, cement industries etc.; and
(ii) Construction industries, such as bridges, dams, and roads etc.,
(iii) Territory Industries: Service industries like the hotel industry, the tourism Industry etc.,
2. Commerce: Commerce is a branch of business. It is concerned with the exchange of goods and services. It includes all those activities which directly (or) indirectly facilitate the exchange.

16.1 Sole proprietorship (single ownership)
A sole proprietorship is the oldest form of business organization. It is found in every country in the world. It is easy and simple to organize. The person who contributes capital and manages the business is called the sole trader (or) sole proprietor. It is also known as an individual proprietorship (or) a one-man business.
The sole proprietorship is a form of organization in which an individual invests his entire capital, uses his skills and is solely responsible for the result of his business. He alone owns and controls the business and bears its profit and loss. The only legal formality is to get a licence from the local authority viz, municipality, panchayat etc.. for running a certain type of business
(Eg: Commercial shops, printing presses, fancy shops, etc.)
Definition of Sole Proprietorship:
According to Wheeler, "the sole proprietorship is that form of business (or) ganization this is owned and controlled by a single individual. He receives all the profits and risks all of his property in the success (or) failure of the enterprise."
Advantages of Sole Proprietorship:
The following are some advantages of a sole proprietorship:
1. It is simple and easy to start a business.
2. It requires less capital
3. Complete control over the business
4. It is easy to make decisions on the spot
5. Business secrets can be maintained.
6. Good relations can be maintained with the consumers. 7. Scope for enjoying all the profits from the business
8. No legal formalities.
9. No regulations and restrictions from the government
10. It is easy to windup the business.
Disadvantages of Sole Proprietorship:
1. Limited managerial ability
2. Unlimited liability in case of loss
3. Risk in business is more
4. Uncertain continuity of business
5. The risk of loss of business is faced by the owner only Partnership
A partnership is an association of two (or) more persons to carry out a business and share its profits and losses. The partnership may come into existence either as a result of the expansion of the sole-trading concern (or) through an agreement between two (or) more persons desirous of forming a partnership.
Thus, the partnership is said to be, "that form of business organization in which partners agree to share the profits of a lawful business, managed and carried on either by all (or) by any one of them acting for all."
Definition of Partnership:
• According to Section 4 of the Indian Partnership Act of 1932, the partnership is "the relation between persons who have agreed to share the profits of a business carried on by all (or) any of them acting for all."
PARTNERSHIP DEED:
The partnership deed is also known as a Partnership agreement' (or) 'Articles of partnership'. A partnership firm can be formed through an agreement among two (or) more persons. In India, this agreement may be oral (or) in writing. But it is desirable to have it in writing to avoid any misunderstanding among the partners in the future. All the terms and conditions of the partnership are included in the agreement.
Definition of Partnership Deed:
Partnership deed may be defined as a "Partnership deed is a document containing all the matters according to which mutual right, duties and liabilities of partners in the conduct and management of the affairs of the firm are determined."
TYPES OF PARTNERS:
Various types of partners are in a partnership firm, they are as follows:
1. Active partner: An active partner who takes an active part in the management of the partnership firm is known as an active, working, managing, (or) general partner. His liability is unlimited.
2. Sleeping Partner (or) Dormant Partner: The sleeping (or) dormant partners who merely contribute capital and do not take active interest in the conduct of the business of the firm are called sleeping (or) dormant (or) financing partners.
3. Nominal Partner: Nominal partner who neither contributes capital nor takes any part in the management of the firm. He lends his name to be used as a partner in the business to increase the reputation of the firm.
4. Partners in Profits: A person who shares the profits of a firm but does not share the loss is called a 'partner in profit only'. Usually, he has no right/control in the management of the firm. But his liability to third parties is like other partners, Le., unlimited,
5. Partner by Estoppels: A person may not really be a partner in the business. But by his behaviour, he makes outsiders believe that he is a partner in the business. Such a person is known as 'partner by estoppels'.
6. Secret Partner: The position of a secret partner lies between an active and sleeping partner. His membership in the firm is kept secret from outsiders. His liability is also unlimited.
7. Minor as a Partner: Section 30 of the Partnership Act of 1932 provides that a minor cannot become a partner because he is incapable of entering into a contract. He may, however, be admitted to the benefits of partnership with the consent of all partners. 8. Partner by Holding Out: A person who is actually not a partner, but is declared as a partner and does not stop this publicity even after becoming aware of it, is known as a 'partner by holding out'.
ADVANTAGES OF PARTNERSHIP:
The following are the advantages of partnership:
1. Easy to Form: Formation of a partnership is easy. No formal documents are required to be prepared, except a simple agreement among the partners is sufficient to start a partnership firm.
2. Registration is Not Compulsory: A partnership firm registration is not compulsory. It is left to the discretion of the partners.
3. Larger Financial Resources: As a number of partners contribute to the capital of the firm, it is possible to collect larger financial resources compared to the sole proprietorship. 4. Sharing of Risk: The risk in a business is shared by more people. The burden of
every partner will be much less than compared to the burden of a sole trader.
5. Quick and Better Business Decisions: In partnership, every partner has the right to be consulted. Hence, business problems can be thoroughly discussed, and the best decisions can be made.
6. Close Supervision: Partners have direct access to the employees of the firm and can encourage them for more production.
7. Secrecy: A partnership firm is not required to publish its accounts, so maximum secrecy can be maintained.
8. Greater Managerial Talent: The partners may be assigned duties in accordance with their talent and experience.
9. Protection for Minors: Minors can be admitted into a partnership to share profits. Their liability remains limited up to the extent of their investment.
10. Greater Flexibility: By mutual consent, the partners can change the nature of the business as easily as they like. So the business can always be responsive to changing needs. Its price policy, capital, profit sharing ratio etc., can be changed easily.
11. Easy Dissolution: The partnership can be easily dissolved on insolvency (or) death of a partner. No legal formality is required during dissolution. Disadvantages of Partnership:
1. Limited Finance: The financial resources of a partnership firm are limited to the share
of partners only. The borrowing capacity of the partners is also limited
2. Unlimited Liability: The liability of every partner is unlimited. In case of loss in business, the partner's personal assets are also liable if the property of the firm is not sufficient to repay the debt of the firm.
3. Degree of Secrecy: The degree of secrecy is less in partnership firms. Every matter of business is required to be disclosed to each partner. 4. Uncertain Life: The life of a partnership firm of the business organization completely depends on the life of the partners. In case of death, insolvency etc., of a partner, the partnership firm may dissolve.
5. Lack of Public Faith: partnership firms are free from government restrictions. Partnership firm accounts are not required to be disclosed to the public. Therefore, the public may not have much confidence and faith in this business.
6. Restriction on Transfer of Shares: Partners are not allowed to transfer their shares just like in companies and to leave the firm at their intention, for which, they have to take the consent of other partners of that firm.
7. Delayed Decisions: Each partner's consent is required for taking important decisions in the firm. This result in a delay in the decision-making process, and sometimes this.. delay may lead to losses.

Public Sector Organizations (or) Enterprises:
Government sector organizations are also known as 'public sector undertakings." Government sector organizations are owned, managed and controlled by the central, state (or) local government and are termed as public sector enterprises (or) public enterprises. They produce the goods and services required by society with a service motto. Public sectors are accountable to the parliament and state legislature.
Public sector organizations consist of nationalized sector enterprises such as, banks, Life Insurance Corporation of India and the new enterprises set up by the government, such as Hindustan Machine Tools (HMT), Gas Authority of India (GAIL), and State Trading Corporation (STC) etc.,
Definition of Public Sector Organization/Enterprise :
• A public sector enterprise may be defined "as any commercial (or) industrial undertaking owned and managed by the government with a view to maximize social welfare and uphold the public interest."
Types (or) Forms of Public Sector organizations:
There are three different forms of organization used for the public sector enterprises in India.
These are as follows:
1. Departmental Undertakings: Departmental undertakings are the oldest among public enterprises. A departmental undertaking is organized, managed and financed by the government. The controlling ministry takes all policy matters and other important decisions. The Parliament lays down the general policy for such undertakings. Such organizations function under the overall control of a ministry of the government and are financed and controlled in the same way as any other government department. This form is considered suitable for activities where the government desires to have control over them, considering the public interest, and is primarily used for the provision of essential services such as railways, postal services, broadcasting services etc.,
2. Public Corporations: It is also known as a 'statutory corporation'. Public corporations which have been incorporated under the special acts of the Parliament/State legislative assemblies. Its capital is totally provided by the government. Its management pattern, its powers and functions, the area of activity, rules and regulations for its employees and its relationship with govemment departments, etc., are specified in the concerned act. Some examples of statutory corporations are like, State Bank of India (SBI), Life Insurance Corporation of India (LIC), Industrial Finance Corporation of India (IFCI), State Trading Corporation (STC) etc.,
3. Government Companies: As per the provisions of the Indian company's act, a company in which the Central and/or State government holds 51% (or) more of its capital is regarded as a government company. These companies are registered under the Indian Companies Act, 1956 and follow all those rules and regulations as are applicable to any other registered company. The Government of India has organized and registered a number of its undertakings as government companies to ensure managerial autonomy, operational efficiency and provid competition to the private sector. Some examples of government companies are Hindustan Machine Tools Limited (HMT), Steel Authority of India Limited (SAIL), Indian Oil Corporation (IOL), Hindustan Aeronautics Limited (HAL), Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited (BHEL), Hindustan Shipyard Limited etc.,
Co-Operative Societies:
A co-operative society (or) organization is one which has been voluntarily formed by some people for the promotion of their common economic interest. It is a voluntary association of usually economically weaker sections of society who join to achieve a common objective by fighting against some social evil-through working collectively according to established principles of co-operation. A co-operative society must be registered under the Co-operative societies act, 1912 (or) under a State co-operative societies act.
To start a co-operative society, the workers of any organization can join, collect share amounts ranging between Re.1/- to Rs.10/- and submit the filled-in application to the registrar of co-operatives. The Registrar will depute his officials to visit the first general body meeting, where bye-laws are formed, and the directors are elected at the meeting. After all the formalities are completed and satisfied by the officials of a registrar of co- operatives, then a licence will be issued by the Registrar and thus the society is formed and comes into existence effective from that date.
Definition of Co-operative Societies:
"A co-operative society is a form of organization wherein persons voluntarily associate as human beings on the basis of equality for the promotion of the economic interests of themselves." -H.C. Calvert.
Types of Co-operative Societies:
The following are some co-operative societies listed below:
1. Co-operative Housing Society: It proved to be very useful to low and middle income groups who cannot afford to construct houses. These co-operative housing societies provide loans to their members and the houses are built according to the needs of their members. Land and building materials can be acquired at lower prices. br 2. Consumer Co-operative Society: The Consumer co-operative society's main objective is to avoid the middleman by directly purchasing the products from the producers and distributors at reasonable prices for its members. This may be in the form of wholesale (or) retail type. Ex: Consumer co-operative stores, super bazars, etc.,
3. Industrial (or) Producers Co-operative Society: These societies are formed to help the craftsmen and skilled workers engaged in cottage (or) small-scale industries. The main purpose is to improve the economic and social conditions of the people by giving them necessary help in the manufacturing of goods and rendering services.
4. Co-operative Credit Society: A co-operative credit society is generally formed by the employees of one company (or) group of employees. Its main objective is sanctioning loans for the development of agricultural land, and purchase of any agricultural machinery and equipment (or) household appliances. These are two types, i.e., (i) Agricultural credit co-operatives, and
(ii) Non-agricultural credit societies.
5. Co-operative Farming Society: It is a voluntary association of farmers who pool their land and other resources with a view to obtaining the advantages of large holdings and scientific methods of cultivation. It includes several types: joint farming society, tenant forming society, barter farming society etc..


6. Co-operative Marketing Society: Co-operative marketing society is an organization that formed to sell the products of its members. The activity undertaken by such societies include grading, warehousing, transportation, storage, financing. Its main aim is to secure a remunerative price for the surplus produce of the farmers.

(i) Limited liability of its members.

(v) It has lower operating costs.

(1) It has limited capital.
